Case Study

Green Aisle – Designing a Companion App for Ethical Consumers

Our daily food consumption is causing a variety of environmental and social issues. Many consumers want to reduce their impact by purchasing more ethical products. However, they often fail at doing so, as they face a variety of challenges. The objective of this project was to design an app that addresses these challenges and assists people in consuming more ethically. The design of the app is informed by the research and insights I’ve gained through my masters’ thesis.

Introduction

We Need to Change What We Eat

Our generation faces a variety of unprecedented environmental and social issues. While the consequences of climate change are getting worse with every day, many companies continue to pollute the environment, pay their workers poverty wages, and stash their profits in offshore tax havens.

Unfortunately, the food industry is no exception. With its massive carbon footprint, unsustainable farming practices, and unethical company politics, the industry is contributing to many of these issues. Therefore, we have to fundamentally change how and what we consume. We have to carefully choose what we buy and look behind the brands, so we can choose products that have a positive impact on people, the planet and animals.

The Problem

The Intention-Behaviour Gap

More and more consumers want to reduce the impact of their consumption by purchasing more ethical products. However, they often fail at doing so, as they face a variety of challenges. Studies revealed that only 10-15% of the people who intend to consume ethically, actually purchase ethical products. In scientific research, this problem is referred to as the intention-behaviour gap.

This project aims to support consumers in bridging the intention-behaviour gap by overcoming the challenges of ethical consumption.

Understanding the Problem

Challenges of Ethical Consumers

The reasons for the existence of the intention-behaviour gap are equally complex and multi-faceted as ethical consumption itself. For instance, it requires a lot of time to do the required research, the willingness to compromise and sacrifice existing comforts, as well as the willpower to stick to the newly established shopping routines.

Through my research, I’ve gained insights into these challenges and struggles of over 100 ethical consumers. When I categorised these problems into different groups, four main problems became apparent.

Research Insight
Complex Ethical Consumption Issues

Consumers stated that it requires a lot of time and energy to understand ethical consumption issues, as these issues are often complex and information widespread.

Price & Availability

Many consumers struggled with finding ethical alternatives as they are often not available in their supermarket or simply too expensive.

Lack of Planning

Consumers often don’t plan their shopping trips. In the supermarket, they end up being overwhelmed by the amount of choice or not having enough time to find an alternative.

Deceptive Practices

Consumers are often tricked into buying supposedly ‘ethical’ products with deceptive marketing practices, fake eco-labels, or complicated industry jargon.

Opportunities

How an App Can Help

By combining the qualitative insights from my study with the theoretical research of the intention-behaviour gap, I developed a set of guidelines specifying how an app can effectively support users in bridging the intention-behaviour gap and consuming more ethically.

Design Guidelines
1
Support Progressive Learning

Enable users to progressively learn about ethical consumption issues by providing compact pieces of information along with clear suggested actions.

2
Personalize Product Recommendations

Provide personalized product recommendations based on the individual needs of the user, such as diet, place of residence, or financial means.

3
Encourage Creation of Implementation Plans

Encourage and support users to create concrete action plans that specify how they will implement an ethical intention in the future.

4
Sustain New Behaviour

Rewards users for their new ethical shopping behaviour and show the impact their actions to sustain a long-term behaviour change.

Design Process (1/3)

Analysing the Competition

As one of the first steps of the design process, I had a closer look at similar apps and services that also aim at supporting people in consuming ethically and healthy.

I found that most of the apps only present information on either the ethicality or the healthiness of a product or brand to the user. Besides that, the user does not receive any other support in changing their consumption behaviour.

Design Process (2/3)

Exploring the User Personas

By talking to a variety of ethically-minded consumers and through the qualitative data of my study, I've collected a variety of different user characteristics and use cases for the app.

Throughout this process, I’ve also realized that there are many cultural differences in our food consumption that are often not considered. For instance, none of the apps I've tested before, allowed to set the dietary preferences to kosher or halal.

I've attempted to capture all of these differences in seven users personas, to help me to consider the individual needs and wants of the different user groups throughout the design process.

Design Process (3/3)

From User Stories to Wireframes

After finishing the initial research phase, I started writing user stories and created a high-level user flow. Based on the user flow and the user stories, I then created wireframes for the individual screens. I used those wireframes to create a simple prototype and run user tests to get some early feedback.

Final Design

Introducing Green Aisle

After integrating the feedback from the user test into the wireframes, I moved on to designing the UI. In the following, some of the core features of the app are highlighted.

Onboarding

The onboarding does not only introduce the app to the users. It also includes three short questions that allow the app to already give personalized recommendations the first time the user is opening the app.

Challenges

Challenges provide compact and actionable information on ethical issues. They are divided into short sessions, through which users can progressively learn about the issue.

Sessions

Sessions are short 1-3 minute units that focus on a specific topic. They can have different formats, such as testing your knowledge about an issue with a quiz (as shown in this example), exploring ethical alternatives for a specific product, or forming implementation intentions.

Product Detail Page

The product detail page shows information on the ethicality and healthiness of a product. Users can get a quick overview of the most important information, deep-dive into the individual ratings, and find personalized recommendations for alternative products.

Adaptive Start Screen

The start screen is growing over time together with its user. In the beginning, for instance, it encourages the user to complete their profile to further personalize their experience.

Later on, the start screen is focussing on supporting users in their current challenge and providing helpful information by the community.
