Case Study

Clothparency – Designing Intuitive and Trustworthy Ratings

Clothparency is a french startup that aims at changing the fashion industry by bringing more transparency to it. They provide information on the ethicality of clothes to the end-consumers so that they can make reasoned purchase decisions. I collaborated with their team to improve the user experience and design of their app. I specifically focussed on the rating system to make it more trustworthy and intuitive to understand. The main challenge of this project was to develop a solution that is only using the existing resources and can be easily implemented.

Introduction

The Need for Ethical Clothes

Clothes are a fundamental part of our everyday life. They are worn by almost everyone, nearly all the time. However, the way how we produce and use clothes is extremely harmful to the environment and people.

The production requires large amounts of nonrenewable resources while the workers are often exposed to dangerous environments and inhumane conditions. On top of that, the textile industry causes water pollution, massive greenhouse gas emissions, animal abuse, forced labour and even child labour. And all of this, only to produce clothes that are often worn for only a short period, after which the materials are largely lost to landfill or incineration.

Meet Clothparency

Clothparency Is Here to Help

Clothparency aims at transforming the textile industry by empowering consumers with data. They provide information on the environmental and social impact of clothing products to enable consumers to make a reasoned purchase decision. Users can either browse an extensive catalogue of brands and products or scan the barcode of a product directly in the store.

As part of my Master's programme, I collaborated with Clothparency for five weeks to improve the user experience and design of their product. Since I only had a few hours a week for this project, the first challenge was to find an area in which I could effectively support the team with my expertise. After an initial meeting, we decided to focus on the rating page of the app.

The Old Rating Page
User Experience Review

Reviewing the Rating System

As an initial step, I conducted a user experience review of the existing rating page to detect possible usability issues. I noticed, that one major area of improvement was the rating system itself. Since it’s a core element of the whole app, I decided to focus my attention on this issue.

After researching best practices, analyzing other apps and websites, and looking into psychological research on rating systems, I developed a series of possible improvements. Both, the identified usability issues and the suggested improvements, are shown below.

Usability Issues of Rating System
1
Alphabetical Scales Are Unintuitive and Culture-Dependent

Someone who is being told that a shirt has a “C” on Clothparency would not know if this a good or bad rating. Someone from the United States might assume the rating ranges from "A" to “F”, whereas users from countries who do not use the Latin alphabet might not understand the rating at all.

→ Use a numbered rating systems. They are more intuitive to understand and (mostly) culture-independent.

2
3-Item Scales Are Too Vague

Only having three different possible ratings can be very limiting, since the rating can fail to represent smaller differences between products and thereby make it harder to compare them.

→ Use a 10-item scale. Thereby, the rating provides a more nuanced evaluation and improves the comparability between products.

3
Ratings Lack Context

Many customers can’t afford completely ethical clothes, as they often come with a price premium. That’s why Clothparency wants to enable its users to find the most ethical product within their budget. However, right now it's not possible to tell whether a product is doing better or worse than similar products in its price range.

→ Use a 10-item scale. Thereby, the rating provides a more nuanced evaluation and improves the comparability between products.

4
Categories Do Not Help With Comparing Products

Ratings of products from the same brand can differ significantly due to the usage of different materials. At the moment, Clothparency is grouping all insights based on the categories environment, humans, and animals. While these categories do make sense, they make it difficult to comprehend the impact of brands general practices and the specific materials used in the product on the final rating.

→ Group insights based on the categories "brand" and "materials". This enables users to quickly understand the ethicality of a brand and comprehend how material choice is impacting the sustainability of a product.

Sketching & Wireframing

Concept Development

Based on the identified usability issues and possible improvements, I started sketching a variety of new concepts and then created low-fidelity wireframes in Sketch.

Initially, I worked towards developing a scale that includes the absolute rating (i.e. the overall rating of the product) as well as the relative rating (i.e. the rating of the product in relation to other products from the same brand or in the same price range). However, the feedback from a quick user test was very clear: While the provided information added value to the product, they were too complicated to understand.

First Concepts

I went back to the sketching board to find a way to show the relative rating in an easier, less technical way. This eventually led to the idea to show the relative rating along with the most important insights of the product's rating just below the absolute rating.

Some further user testing did not only confirm that this approach was easier to understand but also showed that users appreciated having the most important information highlighted right in the beginning.

UI Design

Designing The New User Interface

After showing the new concept as well as the feedback from the first user testing to the team of Clothparency, we decided to go further with the concept. Hence, I started to apply the visual language of Clothparency to the wireframes and continued to work out the details of each section.

The New Rating System

The new rating system is using a scale from 0-10, with 10 representing the best score. Just below the rating, the most important insights of the product rating are highlighted.

This section usually includes the relative rating, i.e. the information on how the product is performing compared to other products of the same brand or price range.

Dealing With Missing Information

Clothparency is sometimes missing information in certain categories of the rating. Instead of hiding these categories, the new design is now showing to the user where Clothparency is missing information.

Furthermore, in cases where Clothparency has only very few insights at all about a brand or product, no total rating is calculated.

User Testing

Validating New Rating System

To evaluate and compare the new design with the current, I conducted a user test. In this test, the participants were shown three different products on Clothparency and asked to select one of the products based on the information provided.

The test was executed once for each concept (i.e. the current and the new design). To reduce bias the concepts were shown in a randomized order. Both concepts included the same information, just represented in different ways. After each test, the participants were asked to answer a short questionnaire.

Impact

User Test Results

The results of the user test showed promising results. The participants preferred not only the aesthetics of the redesign across the bank but also found the new rating scale more intuitive. Furthermore, the participants praised the key insights section.

However, I also found a few areas of improvement. Some users mentioned that the key insight descriptions are too long. They liked the heading to easily grasp the key insight. The additional description should not be longer than two lines. The option to make the key insights expandable, just like the criteria, was also mentioned.
